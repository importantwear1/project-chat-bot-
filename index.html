Unit I — Entrepreneurship

Core concepts, traits, motivation & types of entrepreneurs

What is Entrepreneurship?
It is the process of starting and running a business by taking risks to earn profit. [Learn More]
Need: Creates jobs, generates wealth, drives innovation, solves social problems. [Learn More]
Scope: Manufacturing, services, trade, agriculture, technology — any sector. [Learn More]
graph LR A[Idea/Innovation] --> B[Risk Taking] B --> C[Resource Organization] C --> D[Value & Profit]
Diagram: The core process of entrepreneurship.
Example (Simple Word Explanation): Think of someone who opens a new type of tea stall where customers can order via WhatsApp. They took a risk (invested money), brought an innovation (WhatsApp ordering), and created value for busy office workers. This is Entrepreneurship!
Entrepreneurial Traits & Competencies
Risk-taking — ready to face uncertainty and financial loss. [Learn More]
Innovation — always looking for new, creative ideas. [Learn More]
Leadership — can guide a team and inspire others. [Learn More]
Decision-making — acts quickly and wisely in tough situations. [Learn More]
Self-confidence & persistence — never giving up after failure. [Learn More]
McClelland's Achievement Motivation Theory
David McClelland said people are motivated by 3 needs: nAch · nPow · nAff [Learn More]
graph TD M[McClelland's Theory] --> Ach[n-Ach: Achievement] M --> Pow[n-Pow: Power] M --> Aff[n-Aff: Affiliation] Ach -.-> |"Entrepreneurs have high n-Ach"| E[Entrepreneurs]
Diagram: McClelland's 3 Needs Theory
n-Ach (Need for Achievement): Desire to do better, set challenging goals. Entrepreneurs have HIGH nAch. [Learn More]
n-Pow (Need for Power): Desire to influence others and have control. [Learn More]
n-Aff (Need for Affiliation): Desire for friendly and close relationships. [Learn More]
Exam tip:

Always explain all 3 needs. Clearly state that entrepreneurs are HIGH in nAch — examiners look for this specific point!

Entrepreneur vs Intrapreneur

Entrepreneur

Starts their own independent business. Bears full risk of failure. Uses their own resources. Takes full profit or loss.

Intrapreneur

Innovates from inside an existing company. Risk is borne by the company. Uses company resources. Gets a salary plus rewards/bonuses.

Classification of Entrepreneurs
Innovative: Introduces totally new products/ideas (e.g., Elon Musk creating Tesla). [Learn More]
Imitative: Copies successful ideas in new markets (e.g., creating a local version of Uber). [Learn More]
Fabian: Very cautious, changes only when forced by competition. [Learn More]
Drone: Refuses to change even when failing or suffering losses. [Learn More]
Social Entrepreneur: Solves social problems rather than just making profits (e.g., Grameen Bank). [Learn More]

Unit II — Entrepreneurial Idea & Innovation

How ideas are born, opportunities found, and value created

Innovation — Introduction
Innovation = converting a new idea into a useful product/service that creates value. [Learn More]
It is NOT just invention. Invention is creating something new; Innovation is making it work successfully in the market. [Learn More]
Example: Inventing a new type of battery is an invention. Putting that battery into a practical electric car that people can buy is innovation.
Idea Generation Methods
graph LR S[Substitute] --> C[Combine] C --> A[Adapt] A --> M[Modify] M --> P[Put to other use] P --> E[Eliminate] E --> R[Reverse]
Diagram: SCAMPER Technique flow for generating ideas
Brainstorming — A group session to generate many ideas quickly without judging them. [Learn More]
SCAMPER — A checklist tool (Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other use, Eliminate, Reverse). [Learn More]
Market survey — Talking directly to customers about their unsolved daily problems. [Learn More]
Identifying Business Opportunities
Look for: unsatisfied needs, emerging trends, changing laws, gaps in the market. [Learn More]
Filter ideas by: feasibility (can we build it?), profitability (will it make money?), and competition. [Learn More]
Opportunity ≠ just an idea. An opportunity must be profitable, timely, and possible to execute. [Learn More]
Business Model
A Business Model explains how a company creates, delivers, and captures value. [Learn More]
Key parts: Customer segment (who), Value proposition (what), Revenue streams (how you earn), Cost structure (what you spend). [Learn More]
Exam tip:

Mention the "Business Model Canvas" which has 9 building blocks. Drawing a simple grid for it scores extra marks!

Unit III — Project Management

Project lifecycle, manager's role, and feasibility appraisal

What is a Project?
A project is a temporary activity with a defined start, defined end, clear scope, and a specific goal. [Learn More]
Project Management = planning, organizing, and controlling resources to complete the project successfully. [Learn More]
Project Life Cycle (4 Phases)
flowchart LR I[1. Initiation] --> P[2. Planning] P --> E[3. Execution] E --> C[4. Closure] style I fill:#f9d0c4,stroke:#333,stroke-width:2px style P fill:#f3e8cb,stroke:#333,stroke-width:2px style E fill:#d4e5d4,stroke:#333,stroke-width:2px style C fill:#c6d8e8,stroke:#333,stroke-width:2px
Diagram: The 4 core phases of a Project Life Cycle
1. Initiation — Identify the project idea and check if it is feasible. [Learn More]
2. Planning — Define the exact scope, schedule, budget, and needed resources. [Learn More]
3. Execution — Actual work is done, teams are managed, progress is monitored. [Learn More]
4. Closure — Deliver final output, review what went well, close contracts. [Learn More]
Project Appraisal — Feasibility Report
Before starting a project, we check if it is worth doing. This is called appraisal or feasibility study. [Learn More]
Example (Appraisal for opening a New Cafe):
  • Technical: Do we have the right espresso machines and trained baristas?
  • Market: Do people in this specific area actually drink coffee?
  • Environmental: Are we disposing of plastic cups properly without harming nature?
  • Managerial: Does our team have the experience to run a cafe?
Exam tip:

Write all 4 appraisals (Technical, Market, Environmental, Managerial) with 2-3 lines each for full marks.

Unit IV — Project Financing

Costs, funding sources, capital budgeting & financial statements

Project Cost Estimation
Fixed Capital: Long-term assets like Land, building, machinery — a one-time investment. [Learn More]
Working Capital: Money for day-to-day expenses — raw materials, salaries, electricity bills. [Learn More]
Sources of Funds
Owner's Capital (Equity) — The founder's own money or shares sold to the public. [Learn More]
Debt (Loans) — Borrowed money like bank loans or debentures (must pay interest). [Learn More]
Venture Capital / Angel Investors — Rich investors who fund risky, innovative startups in exchange for ownership. [Learn More]
Capital Budgeting
It is the process of deciding which long-term investment is worth making. [Learn More]
graph TD CB[Capital Budgeting Methods] --> T[Traditional] CB --> D[Discounted Cash Flow] T --> PB[Payback Period] T --> ARR[Accounting Rate of Return] D --> NPV[Net Present Value] D --> IRR[Internal Rate of Return]
Diagram: Different methods of Capital Budgeting
NPV (Net Present Value): Difference between present value of cash inflows and outflows. If positive → accept! [Learn More]
Payback Period: Time taken to recover the initial investment. Shorter is better. [Learn More]
Example (Payback Period): If you buy a machine for ₹100,000 and it saves you ₹25,000 every year, the payback period is 4 years.
Projected Financial Statements
Projected Balance Sheet: Shows expected assets & liabilities at a future date. [Learn More]
Projected Income Statement (P&L): Shows expected revenue, costs, and profit over a period. [Learn More]
Cash Flow Statement: Shows actual cash coming in and going out (liquidity). [Learn More]

Unit V — Management of Enterprises

Management functions, HR, marketing, finance & break-even analysis

Functions of Management
Henri Fayol defined POCCC: Planning → Organizing → Commanding → Coordinating → Controlling. [Learn More]
Modern view: Planning, Organizing, Staffing, Directing, Controlling. [Learn More]
flowchart LR P[Planning] --> O[Organizing] O --> S[Staffing] S --> D[Directing] D --> C[Controlling] C -.->|Feedback| P
Diagram: The continuous cycle of Management Functions
Scientific Management (F.W. Taylor)
Replace guesswork (rule of thumb) with scientific methods. Time and motion studies to find the "one best way" to do a job. [Learn More]
Principles: Science not rule of thumb, Harmony, Cooperation, Max output. [Learn More]
Break-Even Analysis
Break-Even Point (BEP) = the sales level where total cost = total revenue (no profit, no loss). [Learn More]
BEP (units) = Fixed Cost ÷ (Selling Price − Variable Cost per unit) [Learn More]
Example (Break-Even):
  • Fixed Cost (Rent) = ₹10,000
  • Selling Price of 1 burger = ₹100
  • Variable Cost (Bun, patty) = ₹50
  • Contribution per burger = 100 - 50 = ₹50
  • BEP = 10,000 / 50 = 200 burgers. You must sell 200 burgers just to cover costs!
Exam tip:

Always write the BEP formula — even if you don't solve a numerical problem, writing the formula gets marks!

Unit VI — Social Entrepreneurship

Social ventures, innovation, sustainability & legal framework

What is Social Entrepreneurship?
Using business methods to solve social problems rather than just making personal profit. [Learn More]
Goal is Social impact + financial sustainability (earning enough to survive without begging for donations). [Learn More]
pie title Triple Bottom Line of Social Enterprise "People (Social Impact)" : 33.3 "Planet (Environment)" : 33.3 "Profit (Financial survival)" : 33.4
Diagram: The Triple Bottom Line approach
Real World Examples:
  • Grameen Bank: Providing micro-loans to poor people without collateral to help them start small businesses.
  • Aravind Eye Hospital: Providing highly affordable or free eye surgeries to the poor, funded by rich patients paying standard rates (Cross-subsidy model).
Marketing & Risk for Social Ventures
Social enterprises must market their impact and story, not just products to gain trust. [Learn More]
Risks: Funding running out, social resistance to new ideas, "mission drift" (forgetting the social goal and becoming too greedy for profit). [Learn More]
Legal Framework for Social Ventures
Trust — Governed by Indian Trusts Act 1882. Used for purely charitable goals. [Learn More]
Society — Registered under Societies Registration Act 1860. Needs minimum 7 people. [Learn More]
Section 8 Company — A non-profit company under the Companies Act 2013 (like a regular company but profits must be reinvested in the social cause). [Learn More]
